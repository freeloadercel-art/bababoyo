Spatial Analysis of the Holodomor

The Holodomor did not affect all regions of Ukraine equally. Using Geographic Information Systems (GIS), researchers have mapped population losses at the district level, revealing stark spatial patterns. The deadliest areas were concentrated in the fertile black soil belt of central, eastern, and southern Ukraine—where Soviet grain quotas were harshest and enforcement most brutal.

Northern forested regions (Polissia) experienced lower mortality because they were less integrated into the grain economy. Villages on Soviet "black lists" were deliberately cut off from food, creating clusters of extreme suffering. These patterns demonstrate that the famine was targeted, not random.

Key Takeaways:

  • Mortality highest in grain-producing central and southern regions.
  • Villages on Soviet "black lists" were systematically starved.
  • Industrial regions received more state support and saw lower mortality.
  • GIS mapping confirms the famine was targeted, aligning with historical testimonies.

This spatial evidence helps us understand how geography, policy, and ethnicity combined to create one of the deadliest man-made famines in history.
